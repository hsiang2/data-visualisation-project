1. Project Background and Objectives

This project is based on the simulated post-earthquake emergency response in the city of St. Himark. By conducting in-depth analysis of disaster reports submitted by residents, we aim to provide data-driven support for government departments in rescue coordination and resource allocation.

The analysis focuses on three core tasks:

  • 1）Formulating Emergency Response Recommendations: By evaluating the severity of disasters across different communities, the project identifies the most severely affected areas to guide the prioritization of emergency resources and personnel deployment.
  • 2）Uncertainty Analysis of Reported Data: Based on assessments of data completeness and consistency, a reliability score is proposed for each community's reports, enabling decision-makers to gauge data credibility and avoid misjudgments caused by erroneous inputs.
  • 3）Temporal Evolution of Disaster Severity: Using time series analysis techniques, the project investigates the temporal dynamics of the disaster, identifies the mainshock and aftershock periods, and provides insights to inform phased emergency response strategies.

2. Data Missingness Handling and Cleaning Strategy

To ensure the reliability of data analysis, this project adopts differentiated approaches for handling missing values based on the characteristics and missing rates of each field, as detailed below:

  • 1） Creating Indicator Variables: For fields with a high proportion of missing values, such as medical, a Boolean variable medical_reported is introduced, where a value of 1 indicates a valid report and 0 indicates missing data. This approach retains the significance of missingness while allowing the variable to be explicitly considered in subsequent analyses.
  • 2） Median Imputation by Community: For the field shake_intensity, which exhibits a moderate level of missingness and structural differences across communities, median imputation is applied separately within each community.
  • 3） Global Median Imputation: For fields with very low missing rates, such as buildings and sewer_and_water, global median values are used for imputation to avoid introducing significant bias.

Summary: This cleaning strategy strikes a balance between preserving the integrity of the original data and enhancing completeness, laying a solid foundation for subsequent modeling and visualization.

4. Analysis of Missing Data Rates Over Time

We selected six key disaster-related indicators (power, medical, roads_and_bridges, buildings, shake_intensity, sewer_and_water) and plotted the trend of their missing data rates over time.

  • Stable fields: The missing rates for buildings and sewer_and_water consistently remained below 5% with minimal fluctuation, indicating a stable reporting pattern.
  • Volatile fields: The missing rates for power, roads_and_bridges, and medical spiked noticeably during the mainshock and aftershock periods, suggesting that infrastructure disruptions may have impaired reporting capabilities.
  • Case in point: Around 03:00 on April 8, the missing rate for medical surged to 0.42, which aligns with a likely breakdown of the hospital reporting systems immediately following the earthquake.

11. Other Findings and Future Improvements

Findings

  • Severely Affected Areas: It shows significant fluctuations in power issues at Location 4 (nuclear power plant). Power system fluctuations pose a risk of city-wide blackouts. Monitoring should be strengthened and dedicated repair teams deployed, with continuous communication maintained with the plant’s operational departments.
  • Data Quality Variance: While most community data is complete, some areas have high reporting gaps in roads, water, and medical services, leading to uncertainty.
  • Time-Series Characteristics: Reports peaked around April 8, clearly reflecting the main earthquake and aftershock periods.

Future Improvements

  • Handling Potential Collective Exaggeration:
    The issue of potential "collective exaggerated reporting" was not adequately addressed. As noted in related documentation (VAST 2019 - St. Himark - About Our City.docx), location 8, despite being relatively distant from the epicenter, reported higher damage levels than many closer locations. Considering the context that 'Many of the city’s elite and powerful call Scenic Vista [Location 8] home,' one hypothesis is that affluent residents may have a lower tolerance for damage, leading to subjectively inflated or non-objective reporting. This potential bias was not effectively isolated or adjusted for in our analysis. Significant time and effort were invested in exploring this, including analysing correlation coefficients and data variance, but these approaches did not yield satisfactory results in clearly distinguishing or explaining the anomalous reporting pattern of location 8.
  • Reliability Score Limitations:
    The current reliability score, based solely on the average missing data rate per community, is limited in its scope and may not adequately capture the complexities of real-world data trustworthiness. Future iterations should consider additional dimensions (such as data variance, temporal consistency, or cross-validation with other sources) to create a more comprehensive and representative scoring system, thus improving its overall accuracy and applicability.
  • Handling Delayed Reporting:
    The analysis could not account for reporting delays caused by power outages. Several communities experienced periods with zero reports followed by a surge upon power restoration. It was impossible to determine the intended original submission times for these delayed reports. Access to additional information (e.g., outage duration, device offline times) that could allow for the temporal adjustment of these delayed reports would significantly reduce data noise and interference, thereby further enhancing the accuracy and practical utility of the analysis for decision support.
  • Basic Visualization Techniques:
    The types of visualizations employed in the current system are relatively foundational. Future enhancements could explore the use of more advanced visualization libraries or techniques to create more sophisticated and potentially more insightful interactive displays.
